Abstract Art

Abstract art is not a concrete form of an object, but rather a flow of art expressed in pure form elements such as dots, lines, faces, and colors. Because shapes and colors have their own meanings and feelings, they can express the thoughts and feelings of the painter just by matching shapes and colors. After modernism, there was a movement to formally define the world away from decorative art, which became the driving force of abstract art. The history of abstract art dates back to the 19th century, and in 1890 Maurice Deeney's "Painting is a plane covered with colors of order before war horses or knaves" was also a declaration that put an end to the description or narrative expression of things. In the 20th century, Povism promoted the liberation of colors that Gauguin had already claimed, and Quivism decomposed realistic objects and reconstructed them on the screen.

Neo Plasticism Art

Neoplasticism or de Steele is an art movement that began in the Netherlands in 1917. Theologians found a way to express a new utopian ideal of spiritual harmony and order. They supported pure abstraction and universality, which were simplified into essential elements of form and color, simplifying the visual composition vertically and horizontally, and using only black and white and primary colors. In addition, he pursued beauty through the arrangement of lines, colors, and faces, and soon completed a geometric screen with vertical lines, horizontal lines, red, yellow, and blue three primary colors and achromatic colors as basic molding elements. The asymmetrical but harmonious and balanced composition of intersecting lines and primary colored surfaces, and the full-scale and equilibrium screen without the main and wealth are also characteristics of the work of the neologism period.

expressionism Art

Expressionism was the first term used by German critics in 1911 to describe the work of the Beast, early Cubism, Impressionism, and several other artists who consciously refused to imitate nature. I tried to create art as an extension of subjective reality. It was a reaction to bourgeois perception of reality and conventional art, and it was also a reaction to impressionism that depicted the momentary impression of the material world. Characterized by 'distortion' and 'exaggeration' in expression. Trying to express the meaning or essence of an object that is understood as an emotional and mental experience without being caught up in the external surface of a phenomenon that evokes visual interest or reproduction. Ignoring the laws of perspective, anatomy, lighting, and shading, it emphasizes the expressiveness of lines in line drawings or contours, and uses a small number of intense colors that focus on primary colors rather than subtle shades to provide a contrast effect between simple colors or colors.

Futurism Art

Futurism is an art movement founded in 1909 by the Italian poet Philippo Tomaso Marinetti. Denying the traditions of the past and praising the speed and machinery of modern civilization was the essence of the city. Although short-lived, Umberto Bochoni, Boris Pasternak, and Vladimir Mayakovsky are representatives of the futuristic movement. The futurist act was practiced throughout the whole field of art. Futurist painters tried to grasp and describe the movement of the object through multiple viewpoints and multiple viewpoints without fixing the viewpoint. In addition, it tried to put the audience in the center of the screen by synthesizing what they are watching and remembering while paying attention to the afterimage of objects.
