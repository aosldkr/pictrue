Modern Art

Modern Art

Modern art refers to art from the 1860s to the 1970s. It includes works of art from around the 1860s to the 1970s, and also refers to the forms and philosophies of art that arose during this period. Modern art is usually related to art that pursues the spirit of modernism experimentation and abandons the tradition of the past. Modern artists experimented with new perspectives and fresh ideas about the nature of art's materials and roles. It is the characteristic of more modern art to move away from the description, which is a characteristic of traditional art, and to aim for abstraction. And modern art developed into neoclassicalism, romanticism, realism, and impressionism.

Neo classicism Art

It is an art style inspired by ancient Greek and Roman culture that began in England in the late 18th century and appeared throughout Europe, mainly in France. In opposition to the sensuous and colorful trends of previous period art Rococo and Baroque styles, it emphasized intellectual, rational, and moral art. It features classical themes such as myths, Scriptures, and historical events, balanced composition, emphasis on shipping elements, clear historical evidence in architectural background and costume representations, and smooth surface descriptions to the extent that brush marks are not visible.

Romanticism Art

Romanticism arose in opposition to the rationalism of neoclassicism, placing importance on subjectivity rather than objectivity, emotion over reason, and emphasizing freedom rather than law, respecting individuality, and admiring infinite things. Romantic artists protested against the hard and demanding norms of neoclassicalism before and after the Great Revolution, culminating in the enactment of Academyism, especially Napoleon. In other words, romantic artists abandoned Greek and Roman classics to pursue medieval and Gothic styles, and sublimated orientalism to more than just exotic tastes. In romantic painting, the method of painting, that is, the subjective method or subjective expression, was more important than the subject matter of painting.

Representative Writer

Francisco Goya , Caspar David Friedrich ,
Eugène Delacroix , Théodore Géricault

Realistic Art

Realism is a French art trend that rose to its peak in the 1850s by Gustave Courbet (1819-1877) and Honore Daumier (1808-1879), contrary to neoclassical or romanticism. Realist painters refrained from expressing their emotions by breaking away from traditional themes, and realistically portrayed daily life and contradictory events that took place before their eyes. Kurbe expressed his artistic will by saying, "I can't draw an angel because I've never seen an angel," and when his work at the World Expo was rejected in 1855, he built a tent near the exhibition hall and set up a sign of Realism at the entrance to declare realism for the first time. Domier, who was also a realist painter and engraver, satirized the immorality of French society under the theme of corrupt politics and the poor in France in the 19th century.

Representative Writer

Gustave Courbet , Édouard Manet ,
Jean-François Millet , Ilya Yefimovich Lepin

Impressionism Art

Impressionism or impressionism is an art trend that rejects traditional painting techniques and pays attention to color, color tone, and texture itself. Painters who pursued impressionism are called impressionists, who tried to accurately and objectively record the visible world using the instantaneous effects of color or color, while describing nature in changes in color and color. In the 1860s, artists in Paris began to lead. The name Impressionism originated from Claude Monet's oil painting "Impression, Sunrise", which was first used in criticism by critic Louis Leroy in the journal "Le Charivari". Impressionist art influenced the development of impressionist music and impressionist literature.

Representative Writer

Claude Monet , Auguste Renoir ,
Edgar Degas , Paul Cézanne

Neo impressionism Art

Neo-impressionism is a French painting movement or style of the late 19th century that attempts to support the theory and scientificity of impressionism. It is also called the New Impressionists. It was influenced by optical theory or color theory such as Charles Henry (1859-1926), and its biggest feature in technology is that visual mixing is thoroughly carried out by uniform divided brush strokes.It was the first clear appearance during the last Impressionist exhibition in 1886, and Soura and Sinac are representative beings. The New Impressionists made the Impressionists' theory of color more scientifically clear, and they produced extremely theoretical production centered on Seora.
